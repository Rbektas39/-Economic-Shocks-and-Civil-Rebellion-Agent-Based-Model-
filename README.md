# 🌍 Economic Shocks and Civil Rebellion (Agent-Based Model)

This project explores how economic instability influences civil unrest using an agent-based modeling framework. Building on Robert Epstein’s (2002) model of civil violence, I introduce time-varying economic shocks to examine how changes in economic conditions affect the likelihood and intensity of rebellion.

---

## 📊 Overview

Traditional models of civil conflict often focus on static conditions such as poverty or inequality. This project instead focuses on **instability over time**, asking how sudden economic shocks—rather than baseline hardship—shape collective behavior.

Using an agent-based model built in NetLogo, I simulate how individuals respond to changing economic conditions and how these responses aggregate into large-scale patterns of unrest.

---

## 🔍 Research Question

How do the **frequency and magnitude of economic shocks** affect the emergence and intensity of civil rebellion?

---

## 🧠 Key Idea

Rather than treating economic hardship as constant, this model introduces **dynamic shocks** that periodically alter agents’ economic well-being.

The central hypothesis is:

> It is not just poverty that drives rebellion, but **instability and unpredictability** in economic conditions.

---

## ⚙️ Model Framework

This project extends Epstein’s civil violence model with the following components:

### Agents
- Represent individuals in a population  
- Characterized by grievance, risk aversion, and economic condition  

### Environment
- Spatial grid where agents interact locally  
- Law enforcement presence affects perceived risk  

### Behavior Rules
- Agents decide whether to remain inactive or become rebellious  
- Decisions depend on:
  - perceived grievance  
  - expected cost (risk of punishment)  
  - current economic conditions  

### Extension: Economic Shocks
- Introduces **time-varying economic shocks**  
- Shocks affect agents’ economic status and grievance levels  
- Shock parameters include:
  - frequency  
  - magnitude  

---

## 🛠️ Tools Used

- NetLogo (agent-based modeling)  
- BehaviorSpace (simulation experiments)  
- Data analysis tools for interpreting simulation outputs  

---

## 🧪 Methodology

1. Implement baseline Epstein model  
2. Introduce economic shocks as a dynamic variable  
3. Run simulations across different shock scenarios  
4. Use BehaviorSpace to systematically vary:
   - shock frequency  
   - shock magnitude  
5. Analyze resulting patterns in rebellion:
   - frequency of uprisings  
   - intensity of unrest  
   - volatility over time  

---

## 📈 Key Findings

- Economic shocks significantly increase the likelihood of rebellion  
- Frequent shocks lead to **more volatile and unpredictable patterns** of unrest  
- Larger shocks produce **more extreme rebellion events**  
- Instability plays a stronger role than constant hardship in generating large-scale unrest  

---

## 📊 Interpretation

The results suggest that:
- Populations may tolerate consistent hardship  
- But **sudden disruptions** in economic conditions can trigger collective action  
- This helps explain why unrest often follows economic crises rather than long-term poverty alone
